This fall I’m in Macro Theory at Northeastern, and we just extended the Solow growth model past the “capital and saving only” version from earlier in the term. The textbook setup is in Mankiw: capital, labour, and technology drive output, the basic Solow story shows how saving and investment move the economy, and then you add two forces the model still treats as given from outside—population growth and technological progress. The book is explicit that Solow does not explain why fertility or innovation look the way they do; it takes growth rates like population growth as exogenous and then traces the logic (Mankiw, Macroeconomics, 11th ed., the growth chapters where labour-force growth enters the steady state).

That “given from outside” line is what sent me down a rabbit hole about home. South Korea has spent years in the headlines for birthrates that sit far below replacement. Wire coverage of the annual figures—for example [Reuters on the 2024 uptick](https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/south-korea-birthrate-rises-first-time-9-years-marriages-surge-2025-02-26/)—still describes a total fertility rate on the order of roughly three-quarters of a child per woman, i.e. nowhere near the roughly 2.1 replacement benchmark. So in plain language: the workforce is not growing the way demographers assumed in older projections, and the dependency burden is shifting toward older cohorts. That is a real-country fact pattern; the question I wanted to answer for myself was what the stripped-down Solow machinery actually says about it.

In the model with population growth, you write everything in per-worker terms and get a steady state where break-even investment has to cover depreciation, technological upgrading, and capital widening for new workers. Intuitively, a faster-growing labour force dilutes the capital stock across more people—you need more investment just to keep capital per worker from falling. Turn that around: if population growth slows or turns negative, there is less of that dilution. Holding saving behaviour and technology fixed, the steady-state level of capital per worker—and therefore output per worker—can be higher than it would have been under a baby boom. So the textbook model does not give you a simple “fewer people equals poorer country” result for living standards; for income per capita, the sign on slower labour-force growth can go the other way.

That is a useful check on lazy narratives, but it is also where I have to roll my eyes a little at the model, because Korea is not a two-variable homework problem. The same low fertility that flatters capital-per-worker arithmetic in Solow also means fewer workers to produce, pay taxes, and support pension and health systems, and it interacts with participation, migration, housing, and where productivity actually comes from. None of that is in the baseline exogenous-n story. Technological progress is still “given” in that chapter setup too, so if you care about innovation and scale effects, you are already outside the frame where only saving and population growth pin down the long run.

So my takeaway as a student, not a forecaster: Solow is a disciplined way to see that slower workforce growth changes how much capital widening the economy needs, and that can support higher output per worker in the long run if other things stay put. It does not, by itself, tell you whether Korea should feel good about demographic trends, because the costs show up in fiscal policy, family choices, and labour markets that the chapter abstracts away. The model is a lens; the country is the messy object you look at through it.
