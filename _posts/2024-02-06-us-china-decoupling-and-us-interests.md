Tensions between the US and China have been steadily increasing. Is it in the US’ interest to
decouple from China economically?

## Introduction

The establishment of the People's Republic of China—hereafter, “China”—in 1949 led to a
30-year hiatus in U.S.-China diplomatic and trade relations (Siripurapu, 2022). This ended in
1979 when the U.S. recognized Beijing over the Republic of China—hereafter,
“Taiwan”—initiating a trade relationship worth $4 billion that year ("Taiwan and", n.d.). While
significant at the time, in 2022, U.S.-China trade surged to $690.6 billion, solidifying their
statuses as global economic superpowers with GDPs of $25.46 trillion (U.S.) and $17.96 trillion
(China), respectively (Nagashybayeva & Blarcom, 2019; U.S. Department of Commerce, Bureau
of Industry and Security, 2023; World Bank, ca. 2023). However, since 2008, U.S.-China
tensions have intensified due to numerous concerns: trade deficits, intellectual property theft,
currency manipulation, human rights, and South China Sea disputes (Gatten, n.d.). Despite
decoupling discussions, the deep economic ties between the two countries—including trade, and
investments—underscores a pivotal point: Economic decoupling will jeopardize America’s
economic stability and is not a viable or pragmatic option in the U.S.’s interest.

## U.S.-China Trade

U.S.-China trade is arguably the most economically interdependent dyadic trade relationship
globally. In 2022, the U.S. was the largest importer in the world, and China was its largest import
market at 16.5% of total imports (United States Trade Representative [USTR], ca. 2023a).
Simultaneously, China emerged as the world’s largest exporter, and the U.S. was its largest
export market, absorbing approximately 16.2% of all exports (Workman, ca. 2023). U.S.-China
trade climbed for the third consecutive year, and U.S. exports to China grew by 1.6% to $153.8
billion, while imports from China increased by 6.3% to $536.8 billion. Of the U.S.'s $2.1 trillion
in global exports, 7.5% went to China, and 16.5% of the U.S.'s $3.2 trillion imports were sourced
from China.

Bilateral to total trade proportions yield insightful details regarding relative dependencies. In
2022, the U.S. and China's total goods trade (imports and exports) reached $5.4 trillion and $6.3
trillion, respectively, with bilateral trade at $690.6 billion (U.S. Department of Commerce,
Bureau of Industry and Security [USDCBIS], 2023; He, 2023). U.S.-China trade constituted
12.79% of U.S. total trade versus 10.96% for China. This differential indicates that, in relative
terms, the U.S. has a greater dependency on trade with China than vice-versa. Economic
decoupling could impact the U.S. economy more than China's, potentially causing the U.S. to
lose ground to China in the global economic order.

Furthermore, in 2022, the U.S. trade deficit with China rose 8.3% to $382.9 billion, accounting
for 32.4% of the U.S.'s total trade deficit (USDCBIS, 2023). This is primarily attributed to
China’s comparatively lower living standards and the yuan's partial peg to the dollar, making
their consumer goods unbelievably competitive (Amadeo, 2021). From 2001-2018, the
U.S.-China trade deficit led to a 3.7 million rise in U.S. unemployment ("The growing", 2020).
Tariffs introduced under the Trump administration, and later maintained by the Biden
administration, aimed at mitigating this deficit proved counterproductive: they reduced U.S.
economic growth, caused a surge in unemployment by 245,000 jobs, and escalated bilateral
tensions. In 2021, it was estimated that if the trade war intensifies, the U.S. could see a GDP
reduction of $1.6 trillion over five years, job losses reaching 732,000 in 2022 and 320,000 by
2025, and a decline in real income for U.S. households by $6,400 by 2025 (US-China Business
Council, 2021).

Apple, the world’s largest company by market cap, has a significant economic interdependence
with China. In 2020, China accounted for 20% of Apple's total revenues. The company annually
assembles all its 200+ million iPhones within the country. If Apple's Chinese operations stood
alone, they would rank among the world's top 300 firms by revenue. A key reason Apple's
commitment to China is its ability to tap into a labor pool of 3-4 million workers, offering a
combination of scale, flexibility, and cost-effectiveness that is challenging to replicate elsewhere
(Black & Morrison, 2021).

Given that China is projected to drive a third of the global growth in the coming decade, ensuring
market accessibility and maintaining economic relations is pivotal for both U.S. companies’
international success and the U.S. economy (US-China Business Council, 2021).

Microchips, or semiconductors, are the foundational components in numerous technological
devices, ranging from vehicles and PCs to smartphones and medical equipment. Today, the U.S.
dramatically depends on China and Taiwan for their chips; without them, their technological
innovation will stall (Goldman Sachs, 2023). Economic engagement serves as a form of
diplomatic engagement. Reducing economic ties might exacerbate political and diplomatic
tensions, potentially escalating into more significant conflicts that are unnecessary and
counterproductive for the U.S. in China and Taiwan.

Additionally, U.S.-China foreign direct investment (FDI) dynamics further emphasize discernible
patterns of mutual economic reliance. Table 1 indicates that the U.S. and China have nearly
identical FDI proportions towards each other—1.50% (U.S.) and 1.47% (China)—suggesting
that both States value each other equally as investment destinations.

In 2020, U.S. FDI stock in China totaled $123.9 billion, three times the $38.0 billion of Chinese
FDI in the U.S. Although the U.S. represents 3.83% of China's inward FDI, China barely
accounts for 0.35% of U.S. inward FDI (Table 1). This discrepancy suggests that while China
relies more on U.S. investments than vice-versa, the U.S. has a significantly greater investment
in China, hence carrying more financial risk and facing inherent vulnerabilities.

Yet, FDI isn't merely about capital flow; it signifies a long-term stake in the economic future of
the host country. The U.S. has strategically committed to the world’s biggest consumer class.
U.S.-China decoupling would not only present immediate, severe financial risks for U.S.
investors but also missed market opportunities, enabling other global entities to strengthen their
presence and thus diluting American global influence. Furthermore, while addressing tech and
intellectual property concerns with China is vital, the benefits of tapping into Chinese tech
innovation cannot be overlooked, many of whom are frontrunners in their respective fields
(Chorzempa, 2023). Undeniably, any shift in the economic relationship, especially a decoupling,
will be counterproductive for the U.S.

## Closing Remarks

The deep economic ties between the U.S. and China signify more than just trade figures; they
embody mutual reliance in a globalized world. As illustrated, the depth and scale of U.S.-China
trade relations and the strategic foreign direct investments reinforce the importance of this
partnership, not just in monetary terms but also in economic influence and geopolitical
dynamics. The risks associated with decoupling are vast, from immediate financial
vulnerabilities to a potential loss of global influence for the U.S. While challenges exist, isolating
from China could be more detrimental than constructive. In the intricate dance of global
economies, maintaining this partnership, albeit with its complexities, is paramount for the U.S.'s
long-term interests.
