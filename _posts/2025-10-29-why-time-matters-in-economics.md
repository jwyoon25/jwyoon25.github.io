## Why and how does time matter in economics?

The fundamental importance of time in economics lies in three ubiquitous truths:
1. Action and change transpire with the allocation of time.
2. Time is scarce and irreversible and thus subject to the elements of sooner or later.
3. Given 1 and 2, time is a resource and must be economised.

As articulated by Böhm-Bawerk and later von Mises, Austrian economics posits that time preference is the extent to
which actors inherently favour satisfaction in the nearer future over the remoter. The rate of time preference varies
between people and over time but is always positive. It simultaneously influences the premium of present over
future goods, shaping consumer behaviour (e.g. savings, consumption, investment levels). Lower time preference
rates lead to savings and investment, promoting capital formation and economic growth, while higher rates
encourage immediate consumption and reduce future investment.

The phenomenon of interest rates emerges as the price of time because individuals demand compensation (interest)
for deferring consumption. The time value of money (TVM) asserts that \$1 today is worth more than \$1 in the future
due to its potential earning capacity. In his neoclassical interest theory, Fisher defined interest as “an index of a
community’s preference for a dollar of present over [...] future income” and labelled it the “impatience and
opportunity” theory due to it being a result of the forces of time preference and TVM.

Furthermore, among factors such as risk and heuristics, time preference is a parameter of intertemporal choice.
Discounted utility is fundamentally a theory of time preference, and as this preference applies to utility, the same
discount factor applies across choice domains. Over an individual's lifetime, this influences resource allocation,
e.g., retirement planning, insurance, health, education, etc.

Beyond the individual, time's role escalates in significance at the macroeconomic level. In policy-making, Friedman
postulated that monetary and fiscal policy are subject to time lags from implementation to effect as these policy
changes do not have an instantaneous impact but incur “long and variable lags” before affecting the macroeconomy.
Moreover, policies vary in impact over different time horizons. Short-term policies focus on immediate responses to
fluctuations and recessionary pressures, such as managing inflation or stimulating demand. Simultaneously,
long-term strategies are oriented towards structural changes, such as sustainable growth and improved labour market
efficiency.

TVM extends to discounting future benefits and costs, in fields like infrastructure, environmental, and healthcare
policy. Policymakers employ discounting to evaluate the present value of future cash flows, e.g., in climate policy,
the immediate costs of carbon emission reduction are balanced against the future benefits of lessening climate
change effects. The concept is divided into intragenerational discounting, which focuses on impacts within the
current generation's lifespan, and intergenerational discounting, which considers the effects on future generations.
This ensures policies are evaluated for immediate and long-term sustainability, reflecting the critical role of time in
economics.

Time is the bedrock upon which economic theory is built. Accounting for time preference, time value, and time lags
is imperative from intertemporal choice to macro policymaking. Ultimately, time shapes all economic behaviour,
underpinning savings, investment, consumption, production, and growth across micro and macro domains. Its
omnipresence renders time the alpha and omega of economics.
