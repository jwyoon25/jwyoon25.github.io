This post continues the discussion in [Why Time Matters in Economics]({% post_url 2025-10-29-why-time-matters-in-economics %}), drawing on an earlier paper I wrote that asked the same question from the vantage point of several canonical economists. Here the focus is on how they talk about time—not on repeating every theme from the first essay, but on filling in arguments that deserve their own space.

## Irving Fisher: interest as the bridge across time

Irving Fisher placed time at the centre of how we understand interest. He stressed that economic choices are inherently *intertemporal*: people and firms allocate resources across periods, and they typically value present consumption more than future consumption—an idea we now call **time preference**. In Fisher’s theory of interest, the interest rate is what balances the rate of time preference against the productivity of capital. People will only defer current consumption if the return justifies the wait. That logic is foundational for savings, investment, and how capital markets price claims on the future.

## Milton Friedman: “long and variable lags”

Milton Friedman, a defining figure in the Chicago School and monetarism, also underscored time—especially in monetary theory and policy. He famously described **long and variable lags**: monetary policy does not hit the economy overnight. Rate changes and shifts in the money supply work through the banking system, credit conditions, and behaviour in goods and labour markets before they show up in inflation, output, or unemployment. Fiscal policy, too, unfolds between implementation and effect. Friedman’s point is that policy design must internalise those delays; ignoring them invites mistakes such as over-tightening or overstimulation after the relevant part of the cycle has already passed.

## Time as an economic resource

Beyond the textbook triad of land, labour, capital, and entrepreneurship, **time** itself behaves like a resource—though an unusual one. Three ideas capture why:

1. Time allocation is a precondition for action and change.
2. Time is scarce and irreversible, so choices always have a “sooner or later” dimension.
3. Given (1) and (2), time must be *economised* like other scarce inputs.

## At the individual level: time preference and interest

In the Austrian tradition, Böhm-Bawerk and Ludwig von Mises describe **time preference** as the tendency to weight satisfaction in the nearer future more heavily than satisfaction further off. Rates of time preference differ across people and episodes, but the premium of present over future goods is always positive at the margin. That premium shapes saving, consumption, and investment: lower time preference supports capital accumulation and growth; higher time preference tilts toward immediate consumption. It also underpins the demand for interest—as compensation for postponing consumption—which helps explain why capital markets quote a positive price of time.

## Beyond the individual: macro timing

At the macro level, timing and duration matter for business cycles and for the mix of monetary and fiscal tools. Central banks aim to stabilise inflation and support sustainable growth; whether they adjust rates or use balance-sheet policies, the *when* and *how long* of effects remain bound up with the lags Friedman emphasised. Effective stabilisation policy is as much about the calendar as it is about the size of the intervention.

---

Taken together, Fisher, Friedman, and the Austrian account of time preference illustrate a single theme already argued in the [earlier post]({% post_url 2025-10-29-why-time-matters-in-economics %}): from individual plans to aggregate policy, economics is never only about “what” is produced or traded, but *when* benefits and costs arrive. This follow-up simply makes that claim through the language those traditions use best.
