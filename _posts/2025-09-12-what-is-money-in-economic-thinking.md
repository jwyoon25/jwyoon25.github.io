## 2. What is ‘money’ as a concept in economic thinking?

From cattle, cowrie shells, and deerskin to digital bits and bytes and advanced polymer, the material form of money
has developed drastically over the last decamillennium. Concurrently, the ontology of money has been subject to
debate from Plato and Aristotle to Spengler and Keynes. This discourse is essentially divided into two perspectives:
the materialistic viewpoint, which emphasises the tangible attributes of money, and the immaterialistic viewpoint,
which believes “monetary objects are representatives of value” derived from social constructionism.

At its core, money serves three functions in economics:
1. As a medium of exchange,
2. As a unit of account,
3. As a store of value.

Conceptions of ‘money’ have great implications for policy making, behavioural economics, and price setting. For
instance, Keynes’ view of money as primarily being a store of value in his seminal work The General Theory shaped
Keynesian monetary policy, which in turn was widely adopted by governments (e.g., US, UK) during the Golden
Age of Capitalism. However, Friedman's materialistic approach to money as a luxury good in his theory of the
demand for money influenced Thatcherite monetary policy, which, by the 1980s, rejected Keynesian principles.

At the heart of Austrian economics is methodological individualism, and money is fundamentally seen as a market
creation, not a product of government edict or invention. Menger argued that money evolved naturally from barter
systems as a solution to the inefficiencies inherent in the double coincidence of wants. In this view, money's
primary function is as a medium of exchange, facilitating transactions more efficiently than barter could. Menger’s
subjective theory of value further supports this.

Mises asserts that the value of money is fundamentally linked to its utility as a commodity and its function, thereby
being a store of value. His regression theorem posits that the value of money is rooted in its historical purchasing
power, which in turn is influenced by the amount of money in circulation and the demand for it. According to Mises,
this recursive valuation indicates that money must have originally had value as a commodity itself, similar to how
tobacco or gold has intrinsic utility or symbolic worth.

Cryptocurrencies challenge this view, questioning whether money requires an inherent value for purchasing power
or if speculative forces alone can sustain it. Viewing currency as a commodity with intrinsic value fundamentally
influences economic analyses of market exchanges.

Keynes differentiated money into 'money-proper' and 'money-of-account,' distinguishing tangible currency from its
measurement role. He linked these concepts, viewing money as neutral and transient in a real-exchange economy.
This made him consider money as non-influential in economic decision-making, thus irrelevant to economic
modelling.

The concept of money in economic thought is a dynamic construct shaped by historical evolutions and theories.
Whether viewed as a tangible asset or a social construct, its roles as a medium of exchange, a unit of account, and a
store of value remain central to economic analysis. The divergent views of economists like Keynes, Friedman,
Menger, and Mises highlight its complex nature and influence on policy and modelling. As digital currencies
challenge traditional notions, the understanding of money continues to evolve, emphasising the need for adaptable
economic theories in a rapidly changing financial landscape.
