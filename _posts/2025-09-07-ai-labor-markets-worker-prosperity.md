With the rapid advancement of artificial intelligence, most recently seen in generative AI tools such as OpenAI’s ChatGPT, Google’s Gemini, and Anthropic’s Claude, the fear of job displacement is at an all-time high. Yet, this isn’t new. During the Great Depression, when unemployment peaked at 24.9%, John Maynard Keynes warned of technological unemployment, a “disease” caused by the advancement in labor-saving and automation, he stated, “outrunning the pace at which we can find new uses for labour.” Essentially, Keynes referred to the increase in structural unemployment due to automation and technological advancements alike filling in the demand for labor. However, he stamped the term as “only a temporary phase of maladjustment,” and jobs would return in the long run. In pure theory, the technological process increases efficiency, producing more goods at lower prices, driving up consumer-side demand for cheaper products, and leading to more jobs. Thus, the issue at hand concerns whether technological advancement eliminates jobs more quickly than it generates them. In other words, considering the theoretical process, how quickly would the entire cycle unfold and lead to increased demand and associated job creation following the initial productivity gains?

Today’s AI is akin to the steam engine in the Industrial Revolution of the early 18th century, and many fear that AI automation will render most jobs irrelevant. The lump of labor fallacy reflects the misconception that this “AI Revolution” would replace the labor force. The amount of work to be done is not constant because the economy is ever-changing and fluid, rather than a zero-sum game. For instance, from this fallacy's perspective, jobs can be lost to immigration and population growth. However, in reality, the demand for labor is not fixed. Rather, as it is a valuable resource, changes in one industry are offset by expansion in another existing industry or even the creation of an entirely new industry. Furthermore, the circular flow diagram demonstrates that the size of the economy grows: when more workers enter the economy, their additional earnings get spent on purchasing goods and services, and increased spending raises the demand for those products and the labor required to produce them.

However, that's not really how we even understand this. For starters, automation can be a substitute as well as a complement to labor. How that ends up working out depends on what job you do and what task you automate. It could lead to lower or higher demand for individual jobs.

As a result, labor is not a fixed lump. Rather, labor is determined by the underlying demand for the goods and services produced by the labor. In the long run, the number of jobs will increase with the size of the labor force and the economy.

In the short run, however, this transition is far from frictionless. While aggregate labor demand may rise over time, the reallocation of workers across sectors creates meaningful adjustment costs. Workers displaced from routine or automatable jobs often do not seamlessly transition into newly created roles, particularly when those roles require different or more advanced skills. This mismatch gives rise to structural unemployment and contributes to widening inequality, as those who can complement AI—typically higher-skilled workers—capture disproportionate gains, while others fall behind.

Moreover, AI differs from previous waves of automation in that it increasingly encroaches on non-routine cognitive tasks, affecting not only blue-collar jobs but also white-collar professions such as finance, law, and even aspects of medicine. This broad scope intensifies uncertainty and accelerates the pace of disruption. As a result, the central economic challenge is not whether jobs will exist in the long run, but how quickly workers can adapt to the changing composition of labor demand.

To optimize prosperity for workers and families, policy must focus on facilitating this transition. First, investment in human capital is critical. Governments should expand access to education and vocational retraining programs that emphasize adaptable, AI-complementary skills such as critical thinking, creativity, and interpersonal communication. Lifelong learning systems—rather than one-time education—will become essential in an economy where skill demands evolve rapidly.

Second, strengthening social safety nets can mitigate the short-term costs of displacement. Policies such as wage insurance, unemployment benefits tied to retraining, and portable benefits for gig and contract workers can provide stability while encouraging re-entry into the labor force. These measures help smooth consumption and reduce the social tensions that often accompany technological change.

Finally, policymakers must strike a balance between innovation and regulation. Overregulation risks stifling the productivity gains that AI can deliver, while underregulation may exacerbate inequality and ethical concerns. A targeted approach—focusing on transparency, accountability, and risk management—can ensure that AI systems are deployed in ways that augment, rather than replace, human labor.

Ultimately, AI does not eliminate the need for human work; it transforms it. The trajectory of the labor market will depend not only on technological capabilities but also on the institutions and policies that shape how those capabilities are integrated into the economy. By prioritizing adaptability, equity, and innovation, society can harness AI to expand opportunity rather than constrain it.
