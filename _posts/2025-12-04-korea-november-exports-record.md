I saw this floating around on [X](https://x.com/business/status/1991663386241765691) and got curious enough to actually read the [government write-up](https://english.motir.go.kr/eng/article/EATCLdfa319ada/2433/view). I’m Korean, so export data isn’t abstract for me—it’s the background noise of how people talk about jobs, the won, and whether “the economy” feels okay even when your own rent still hurts.

November was a big month on paper: exports up 8.4% year-on-year to about $61bn, which MOTIR says is a record for any November. Imports barely moved (+1.2%), so the surplus landed near $9.7bn. Year-to-date exports through November also set a record for that stretch. So yeah, it’s not one weird print—it’s been building.

If you ask “what actually moved the needle?”, the answer is almost boring: chips. Semiconductor exports jumped 38.6% to about $17.3bn, which they call an all-time monthly high. Data centres, expensive memory, memory prices up—that whole story. Autos helped too (+13.7%), especially hybrids and regular ICE cars, which still sell.

Meanwhile a bunch of other stuff looked rough. Petroleum and petrochemicals were down double digits—not every Korean exporter is riding the AI boom; some are just dealing with oversupply and softer global prices.

The country mix is where it gets relatable. Exports to the U.S. were basically flat (like -0.2%). Semis and whole cars held up; steel, machinery, and parts got squeezed—tariffs and protectionism showing up where you’d expect. China was up (+6.9%), still buying a lot of what we make. ASEAN too. EU, Japan, Latin America slipped this month—I’m not going to pretend I know exactly why from one table, but it’s a reminder the headline number hides a lot of moving parts.

What I take from it as someone who thinks in econ terms: it’s great for the current account when semis are on a tear, but it’s also concentrated. When the global capex cycle turns, those numbers can swing fast. A chunk of the “value” is price as well as volume, and I’d want finer BOK/customs splits before I’d bet anything on that. And the ministry mentions the new Korea–U.S. strategic investment stuff around autos and parts—so policy isn’t just background noise; it’s part of what exporters are pricing in.

So my casual summary: good month, uneven country. Korea still looks like a place that can ship its way out of a soft patch—as long as memory stays hot and the rest of the basket doesn’t fall apart. I’ll keep watching.
