## A Nuanced Approach to Tackling Income Inequality

Redistributive economic policies address income and wealth disparities with a two-fold objective: providing aid to the less fortunate and ensuring that this assistance does not inadvertently cause broader economic harm. These policies are rooted in equity, aiming to balance economic inequalities stemming from various socioeconomic factors by redistributing resources such as human capital and wealth across society.

## Justifying Redistribution

The notion that assisting the poor serves the general public good is deeply embedded in societal ethics. This concept extends beyond mere altruism; it encompasses the understanding that a society where the basic needs of every member are met is more stable, cohesive, and productive. Economically, when the poor are supported, they become more capable of contributing to the economy as consumers or through increased labor productivity.

John Rawls' purely hypothetical theory of the original position or veil of ignorance posits that social and economic inequalities are often the result of arbitrary circumstances, such as the economic status one is born into, and argues for a societal structure where inequalities are arranged to have the greatest benefit on the least advantaged members of society. According to this view, redistributive policies are not just a matter of charity but of correcting the arbitrary imbalances in opportunities and resources that individuals inherit by the accident of birth.

As philosopher Peter Singer suggests, helping the poor is a moral obligation, and giving money to charity is not an act of generosity but rather a duty. This is based on the principle of moral freedom, that the fortunate are obliged to help others so that they can help themselves. Singer’s theory of ethics states that, within developed countries, failing to help the poorest is ethically indefensible.

In neoliberalism economics, which pushes free-market capitalism and limited state intervention, economic inequality is an inherent result. Fundamentally, redistribution is disincentivizing, but finding a system that minimizes the disincentive while still addressing inequality is necessary. Widening inequality has significant implications for macroeconomic growth and stability: it can concentrate political and decision-making power in the hands of a few, reduce investment, and invoke a suboptimal use of human resources, leading to economic inefficiencies. Additionally, high levels of economic inequality harm economic growth in developed countries.

## The Relationship Between Economic Inequality and Economic Growth

Recent studies suggest an inverse relationship between economic inequality and economic growth in developed countries. A 1% increase in inequality typically reduces GDP by 0.6% to 1.1% in a majority pool of developed countries (OECD countries). Similarly, the CEPR’s analysis indicates that for the average country in their sample (1970-2010), a 1 percentage point rise in the Gini coefficient decreases GDP per capita by about 1.1% over five years, with a longer-term cumulative effect of around -4.5%. This negative relationship appears more pronounced in high- and middle-income countries. For example, in such countries (those at the 75th percentile of initial income), the effect of a 1 percentage point increase in the Gini coefficient on GDP per capita is -5.3%, with a corresponding standard error of 0.8%. Conversely, at the 25th percentile of initial income, the same effect is 2.3%, with a corresponding standard error of 0.6%. This suggests that, in developed countries, higher levels of income inequality are associated with lower transitional GDP per capita growth and have a negative long-term effect on GDP per capita levels.

Additionally, the relationship between income inequality and economic growth for the initial level of inequality is concave. A positive or negative effect depends on the observed income inequality level, and the “slope of the polynomial relationship from positive turns negative at a net Gini of about 27 percent, indicating that the inequality overhang occurs at low levels of income inequality” (net Gini refers to after tax and transfers). As countries become more unequal, they bear a greater negative impact on economic development, and therefore, economic inequality and economic growth share an inverse relationship.

## Current Redistributive Policies & its Issues

In the U.S. today, many different policies and programs tackle redistribution amongst its citizens. These redistributive policies are categorized into three forms: Direct anti-poverty programs, taxation, and government market intervention.

Despite the intention to alleviate poverty, it does not. The system is fundamentally poorly targeted, with a significant portion of the benefits going to non-poor and near-poor citizens, resulting in a leakage of resources that could have been more effectively utilized for those who are truly poor. Moreover, the system often creates disincentives for work and savings for recipients and taxpayers. For instance, the structuring of welfare benefits can discourage recipients from seeking higher income or employment due to the potential loss of benefits, leading to long-term dependency on welfare (a phenomenon known as the welfare trap) and hindering economic mobility. However, and most importantly, despite the significant resources allocated to these programs, income and wealth inequality in the U.S. remains high and continues to rise. Income growth has been rapid for the top 5% of families, further exacerbating this disparity. Between 1962 and 2009, adjusted for both population growth and inflation, although anti-poverty spending increased sixfold, the poverty rate fluctuated and showed little downward trend. Current redistributive policies have been implemented at enormous cost but have not done much to reduce poverty.

## Universal Basic Income v.s. Negative Income Tax

Various proposals for Universal Basic Income (UBI) have gained attention in recent years. Although UBI is not a new concept, Andrew Yang’s UBI proposal, termed the “Freedom Dividend” is a notable example. Yang's plan aimed to implement a value-added tax (VAT) to fund a guaranteed annual income of $12,000 for each adult U.S. citizen. According to the Tax Foundation, with approximately 236 million adult citizens in the U.S., Yang’s "Freedom Dividend" would entail an annual cost of about $2.8 trillion. However, it’s important to note that UBI can be seen as a form of negative income tax (NIT) from a different perspective, and when compared, UBI is much more expensive. Rewording Mankiw’s prompt to illustrate, consider the following two hypothetical balanced-budget plans.

Plan:
1. A universal transfer of $1,000 per month for every person, financed by a 20 percent flat tax on all income.
2. A negative income tax with an income floor at $12000 and a 20% phaseout tax rate, with the breakeven point beyond which benefits reach zero, is $60,000.

Although framed differently, these plans are economically equivalent due to the net payment (taxes less transfer), resulting in the same net income for everyone (see Table 2).

Plan 1 mirrors Yang’s proposal in terms of outcome (providing $12,000 annually to every individual), which would cost $2.8 trillion. A UBI that pays everyone 20% of the average income (Plan 1) accounts for about 15.4% of GDP, while an NIT that replicates the identical outcome (Plan 2) costs only 3.8% of the GDP. This reveals a significant disparity in economic efficiency between UBI and NIT despite their conceptual similarity. Therefore, NIT is a more cost-effective approach.

## An Economically Effective and Viable Approach

The NIT, advocated by Nobel laureate Milton Friedman, proposes a unified system that replaces the current array of complex and overlapping anti-poverty programs like TANF, food stamps, and Social Security with a straightforward, two-part structure: a guaranteed minimum income and a flat tax rate on any additional earned income.

The administrative simplicity of this system is one of its primary advantages. By consolidating various welfare programs into one, the NIT would drastically reduce the costs and complexities. The current system's complexity obscures the true amount of redistribution, as it aggregates multiple programs and converts in-kind transfers to cash equivalents. This simplicity extends to taxpayers and beneficiaries, who would find the incentives clearer, and the redistribution would be explicit and calculable. Such clarity would improve the overall transparency of the government’s redistribution efforts.

Furthermore, the NIT addresses one of the significant pitfalls of the current welfare system: work disincentives. Current programs create a ‘welfare cliff’ or, as mentioned before, ‘poverty traps’, where benefits are abruptly lost as income increases, discouraging recipients from seeking higher income or working more hours. For example, they can go from not working to working a full week without increasing their income as they lose their welfare payments and benefits. On the contrary, the NIT ensures a gradual phase-out of benefits, reducing marginal work disincentives. This more moderate approach would encourage beneficiaries to seek employment or work additional hours without the fear of suddenly losing their benefits.

## Closing Remarks

In summary, negative income tax presents a practical income redistribution approach that addresses the shortcomings of current policies and universal basic income proposals. Through its administrative simplicity, work incentives, transparency, and cost-effectiveness, the NIT assists lower-income groups while avoiding dependency or economic harm to decrease income inequality sustainably.
