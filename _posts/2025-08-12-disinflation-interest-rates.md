## Introduction

The slow deceleration of inflation presents a clear challenge for policymakers. Classical economic theory depicts that central banks should lower interest rates in response to a drop in inflation to encourage borrowing, investment, and general economic growth (Keynes, 1936). Yet, there is often a hesitancy to do so. This essay examines the phenomenon of reluctance, employing various economic frameworks and historical antecedents for analysis. Lowering interest rates reduces the cost of borrowing, spurring consumer spending and corporate investments. This can subsequently promote economic growth and help counteract the disinflationary trend. It is grounded in Keynesian economics principles, which advocate for implementing proactive government interventions to regulate economic swings. Recent research has reaffirmed these concepts, highlighting the effectiveness of monetary easing in stimulating economic activity during periods of low inflation (Blanchard, 2021; Summers, 2020).

## Volcker

Paul Volcker, the 12th chairman of the Federal Reserve (Fed), is an example of the importance of credibility in monetary policy. At first, there was scepticism and ambiguity regarding Volcker's assertive hikes in interest rates, which led to volatility in long-term interest rates and predictions of inflation. However, by demonstrating a solid commitment to controlling inflation, the Fed accomplished the goal of stabilising expectations, although it came at the cost of enduring significant short-term economic difficulties (Goodfriend & King, 1980). This underscores the need for central banks to maintain their credibility while overseeing inflation expectations. Implementing expansionary monetary measures too early in response to disinflation may put the central bank's credibility at risk, leading to increased inflation and long-term economic instability (Bernanke, 2020; Powell, 2021).

The response of the Bank of England to disinflation reflects similar concerns. Following the Brexit referendum, the UK experienced significant economic uncertainty, leading to increased complexity in its decision-making process. While theoretical arguments support the idea of lowering interest rates during disinflation, it is crucial also to consider the need for currency stability and the potential risk of capital flight (Carney, 2020; Bailey, 2021). Their cautious approach during the COVID-19 epidemic highlights the challenges of managing interest rates in a low-interest-rate environment. While rate cuts were implemented to strengthen the economy, concerns about the stability and effectiveness of further reductions persisted (Haldane, 2020).

Policymakers often hesitate to reduce interest rates in the face of disinflation, primarily due to concerns about credibility (Goodfriend & King, 1980). An analysis of the early 1980s during Paul Volcker's stint as Fed Chairman demonstrates that the success of measures to decrease inflation was mainly contingent upon the public's confidence in the Fed’s commitment to controlling inflation. Incomplete credibility, typified by uncertainties among markets and the public about the Fed’s determination, resulted in significant volatility in long-term interest rates and a postponed adjustment of inflation expectations. The suspicion made economic actors cautious, as they worried that the steps to decrease inflation could be reversed, thus causing an increase in inflation. The lack of trust can lead to substantial declines in productivity, as evidenced during the Volcker era. Given the historical context, several individuals may express caution regarding the swift reduction of interest rates, as they fear it may undermine the meticulously built credibility of their efforts to combat inflation. Premature easing of monetary policy could result in a resurgence of inflation, destabilising the economy and reversing the progress of lowering inflation expectations.

## 2023-2024 Disinflation with the ECB

The ECB's reluctance to lower interest rates in response to recent disinflation is motivated by concerns about persistent inflationary pressures, particularly from wage increases. Although inflation rates have decreased since reaching their peak in 2022 and 2023, the European Central Bank (ECB) maintains high interest rates to ensure sustained inflation expectations. This strategy is supported by the underlying belief that monetary policy should remain restrictive until inflation is effectively managed and expectations align with the central bank's objectives (ECB, 2024). The significant impact of wage growth persists since the ongoing corrections to previous inflation rates increase prices. Decreasing interest rates too soon could impede attempts to reduce inflation, leading to a revival of inflationary pressures (Friedman, 1968; Blanchard & Perotti, 2002).

### Anchoring inflationary expectations

The ECB's policy also aims to prevent excessive economic growth and protect against inflationary pressures. By pursuing a rigorous monetary policy described in Fisher's debt-deflation hypothesis (Fisher, 1933), the ECB seeks to stabilise expectations and prevent deflationary spirals. Loosening monetary policy at an inappropriate time could weaken the ECB's reputation for controlling inflation, leading to a resurgence of inflation and economic instability.

The ECB's summer statement for 2024 showcases a conscious effort to maintain a balance in fostering a continued decrease in inflation, managing expectations of inflation, and ensuring that inflation achieves the appropriate level sustainably. The hesitancy stems from concerns regarding exacerbating existing imbalances and potentially implementing negative interest rates. Imposing negative interest rates can undermine banks' profitability and destabilise the financial system (Draghi, 2019; Lagarde, 2021). For example, in the mid-2010s, the ECB introduced negative interest rates to combat low inflation and stimulate economic expansion. However, this method posed significant challenges for European banks, as it limited their profit margins (Claeys, 2020). Moreover, the Eurozone is burdened by inherent issues such as rigid labour markets and differing fiscal policies, which limit the effectiveness of traditional monetary actions.

Despite implementing robust monetary easing measures, several Southern European states have consistently struggled with high levels of unemployment and sluggish economic growth (Stiglitz, 2020). Yet applying this principle in real-world situations is complex, as demonstrated by the strategies employed by the Fed, ECB, and BoE, who must harmonise theoretical models with practical constraints and uncertainties. Historically, the Fed has conventionally decreased interest rates to foster economic expansion. Nevertheless, recent occurrences (specifically the Great Recession and the COVID-19 pandemic) have shown that maintaining meager interest rates for long periods can lead to unexpected outcomes, such as the emergence of asset bubbles and financial instability (Bernanke, 2020; Powell, 2021).

An example of this is the rapid rise in residential property values in the US, which can be attributed, in part, to the protracted period of low interest rates. This has incentivised increased borrowing and investment in real estate (Shiller, 2020). The Fed has adopted a more prudent approach, particularly in assessing disinflation, as it assesses the possible risks associated with a rapid decrease in interest rates. Likewise, the ECB encounters challenges within the Eurozone due to significant variations in economic conditions among member countries. In fulfilling their obligation to preserve price stability, they must consider their member countries' diverse economic conditions and budgetary strategies. During periods of disinflation, the ECB may exhibit reluctance to decrease interest rates, demonstrating its cautious stance in abstaining from swiftly cutting interest rates despite the hint from economic theory that it may be reasonable.

## Closing Remarks

Despite appearing to contradict classical economic theory, the reluctance to lower interest rates in reaction to disinflation is consistent with a more thorough understanding of economic dynamics. Policymakers must diligently assess the prospective benefits of implementing monetary easing compared to the tangible risks of disturbing inflation expectations, inducing financial instability, and diminishing the efficacy of policy measures. Central banks use a cautious strategy when lowering interest rates, guided by past experiences and the significance of upholding their reputation. This technique aligns with a comprehensive viewpoint of economic theory, which considers not only the theoretical advantages of monetary easing but also the concrete hazards linked to such measures.
