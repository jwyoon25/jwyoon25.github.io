## 1. Why is the inflation being experienced by the U.K. proving to be more ‘sticky’ than expected?

Recent years saw a drastic rise in prices around the world. In the UK, citizens live through a cost-of-living crisis.
Why has inflation not cooled as expected, persisted, and remained so ‘sticky’?

So much has developed, both internally and externally, in just the last half-decade that has had significant
implications for the UK economy: Brexit, COVID-19, Russo-Ukrainian War, record devaluation of the pound, Green
Policies, etc. Every cause of inflation has unfolded simultaneously and has operated together: demand increased,
supply decreased, and costs rose.

This paper makes two observations on why inflation has persisted at high rates in the UK:
1. Significant demand-pull inflationary pressures stemming from pent-up pandemic demand.
2. Supply-side cost-push inflation initially from the pandemic was exacerbated by the tight labour market, the
Russo-Ukrainian war and climate policies.

## Observation One

Demand-pull inflation occurs when the aggregate demand in an economy outstrips aggregate supply, increasing the
overall price level. In the context of the economic climate post-pandemic, this phenomenon can be demonstrated
with the quantity theory of money (or equation of exchange). The theory is expressed as:

MV = PY
P = MV / Y

Where M = Money supply; V = Velocity of money; P = Price level; Y = Real GDP. Inflation emerges when the price
level rises. P is directly proportional to M or V and inversely to Y.

During the pandemic, the money supply soared (Fig 3), real GDP contracted (Fig 1), and interest rates saw historic
lows of 0.1% (Fig 2). Growing money supply contributes to low-interest rates, and as cheap credit also leads to the
growth of broad money, the supply of money will recursively increase during periods of low bank rates.

Furthermore, Figure 4 slopes downward: a lower interest rate leads to a lower opportunity cost of holding money
and increases the quantity of money demanded, as does the demand for money. Thus, because the velocity of money
is inversely related to the demand for money, it can be concluded that the velocity of money increased. Revisiting
the equation, the significant increase in the money supply (M), coupled with an increase in the velocity of money
(V), a decrease in real GDP (Y), and constrained supply, increased price levels (P). This significant inflationary
pressure led to a surge in demand that outstripped supply, causing demand-pull inflation after a lag (Fig 3 CPI). This
inflationary pressure was further exacerbated by inflationary expectations entrenched in the economy.

Moreover, “too much money [chased] after too few goods” explains the inflationary pressures that emerged during
and after the pandemic. The UK government engaged in substantial fiscal and monetary stimulus, channelling funds
to household bank accounts. With spending options curtailed amidst lockdowns, household savings ratios (i.e., the
amount of income earned but not spent) reached record highs of 29.1% (Fig 5).

When the economy reopened in 2021, pent-up consumer demand was unleashed, injecting massive amounts of
saved cash into the economy. However, global supply chain disruptions during the pandemic severely hampered
production capacity. Excess liquidity chased scarce goods, putting upward pressure on prices. However, even after
global supply chain disruptions were gone, inflation continued to persist. Why? This leads me to observation two.

## Observation Two

Cost-push inflation is triggered by an increase in the cost of production (due to higher wages and rising prices for
raw materials) and a subsequent decrease in aggregate supply. Ultimately, price increases from production filter onto
consumers, creating inflation.

Raithatha states that UK core services, core goods, food, and energy are all four key elements that contribute
significantly and relatively equally to inflation in the UK, a rather distinct situation. In contrast, in the United States,
the primary factor is core services, whereas in the Eurozone, energy prices were initially the main drivers of
inflation, later supplanted by food prices. The UK is experiencing the worst of both worlds: a labour supply shock
similar to that in the US, coupled with an energy and food price shock akin to that in the Eurozone. This
combination has led to persistently high, ‘sticky’ inflation.

Figure 6: Bernanke and Blanchard Model of Wages and Prices (Stylised)
{% include figure.liquid path="assets/img/posts/uk-inflation-stickiness/fig-p03-01.png" class="img-fluid rounded z-depth-1" zoomable=true %}

Wage growth depends on expected inflation, labour market tightness, desired real wages, and productivity, driving
wage inflation. Firms then price based on wages and costs (e.g., energy), considering productivity and returns,
causing price inflation. This indirectly relates to Observation One: money supply influences demand, impacting
labour tightness and inflation expectations. However, wage-price interaction is bidirectional. Price shocks also
influence wage expectations and feedback to prices, with wage-price dynamics shifting due to lags in wage
adjustments and inflation expectations.

Figure 7: Wage growth and labour market tightness (vacancies-to-unemployment ratio) (1990-2023)
{% include figure.liquid path="assets/img/posts/uk-inflation-stickiness/fig-p04-01.png" class="img-fluid rounded z-depth-1" zoomable=true %}

The V/U ratio increased over decades, correlating with significant wage inflation growth. As the ratio rises,
employers compete more intensely for scarcer workers, elevating wage pressure. Prolonged labour tightness (in
absolute terms) generates faster wage growth and, thus, higher inflation.

Figure 8: Decomposition of price inflation (2020 Q1 to 2023 Q2)
{% include figure.liquid path="assets/img/posts/uk-inflation-stickiness/fig-p04-02.png" class="img-fluid rounded z-depth-1" zoomable=true %}

Annual CPI inflation in the UK began its upward trajectory in 2021, starting at 0.7% at the year's onset and
escalating to 5.4% by year's end. Key drivers of increasing 2021 inflation were energy prices and shortages.
Energy prices began their ascent in Q2 of 2021, well ahead of the Ukraine conflict in Q1 of 2022, due to the Ofgem
price cap increase in April 2021. According to Haskel, disturbances in energy and food prices and shortages during
and after the pandemic are 10.8, 3.4, and 36.1 times greater than the usual pre-pandemic disturbances, and 2021 Q3
had a sizable shortage shock. In Q4, price shocks continued as past shocks still impacted the economy. The labour
market tightened rapidly (Fig 7 V/U ratio spike over this period), moderately increasing inflation. At the same time,
Omicron's emergence created uncertainty, and the furlough scheme closure meant a limited unemployment spike.

As the Russo-Ukrainian war unfolded in Q2 of 2022, energy prices rapidly increased and had lingering shortage
effects. The UK's inflationary response to energy shocks is more pronounced and shows different lags than the
Eurozone because of the UK energy market's unique nature (gas dominance) and regulatory price framework. The
Ofgem price cap in April 2022 contributed to rising energy prices. From 2022 Q3, food prices had a material
inflationary impact, and the tight labour market (see Fig 7) kept pushing inflation up. In early 2023, falling energy
prices dragged on inflation while still-high food inflation persisted, with lingering shortages and tight labour market
effects. Supply shocks still drive inflation, though falling energy partly offsets rising food and shortage effects,
leaving labour tightness as a positive contributor.

## Closing Remarks

The UK's sticky inflation stems from substantial pent-up pandemic demand unleashed when lockdowns eased,
coupled with persistent supply-side shocks. Demand-pull pressures emerged as excess savings chased scarce goods
when the economy reopened. Meanwhile, cost-push shocks from tight labour markets, the Russo-Ukrainian war,
climate policies, and lingering pandemic shortages have repeatedly battered supply. These concurrent demand spikes
and supply slumps explain why inflation has remained stubbornly high despite falling at the margins. Persistently
increasing production costs and embedded inflation expectations ensure inflation remains sticky even as demand
falls. Only by patiently allowing lagged policy impacts to restore supply-demand balance can disinflation hopes
materialise. Time will tell whether inflation or the economy itself proves stickier.
