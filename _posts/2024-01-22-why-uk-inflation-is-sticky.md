## Why is inflation in the U.K. proving more sticky than expected?

Recent years saw a drastic rise in prices around the world. In the UK, citizens have lived through a cost-of-living crisis. Why has inflation not cooled as expected, persisted, and remained so sticky?

So much has developed, both internally and externally, in just the last half-decade that has had significant implications for the UK economy: Brexit, COVID-19, the Russo-Ukrainian war, record devaluation of the pound, and green policies. Every cause of inflation has unfolded simultaneously and has operated together: demand increased, supply decreased, and costs rose.

This paper makes two observations on why inflation has persisted at high rates in the UK:
1. Significant demand-pull inflationary pressures stemming from pent-up pandemic demand.
2. Supply-side cost-push inflation initially from the pandemic, then exacerbated by the tight labour market, the Russo-Ukrainian war, and climate policies.

## Observation One

Demand-pull inflation occurs when aggregate demand in an economy outstrips aggregate supply, increasing the overall price level. In the post-pandemic context, this can be demonstrated with the quantity theory of money (the equation of exchange):

\(MV = PY\)

\(P = \frac{MV}{Y}\)

Where \(M\) = money supply, \(V\) = velocity of money, \(P\) = price level, and \(Y\) = real GDP. Inflation emerges when the price level rises. \(P\) is directly proportional to \(M\) or \(V\) and inversely proportional to \(Y\).

During the pandemic, the money supply soared, real GDP contracted, and interest rates reached historic lows of 0.1%. Growing money supply contributes to low interest rates, and cheap credit can recursively increase broad money growth during low bank-rate periods.

Figure 4 slopes downward: a lower interest rate lowers the opportunity cost of holding money and raises the quantity of money demanded. Because the velocity of money is inversely related to money demand, velocity increased. Revisiting the equation, a significant increase in money supply \(M\), an increase in velocity \(V\), and a decrease in real GDP \(Y\), alongside constrained supply, increased price levels \(P\). This generated inflationary pressure that led to demand outstripping supply, causing demand-pull inflation after a lag. Inflationary expectations then entrenched this pressure further.

“Too much money chasing too few goods” also captures the dynamic during and after the pandemic. The UK government engaged in substantial fiscal and monetary stimulus, channelling funds to household bank accounts. With spending options curtailed during lockdowns, household savings ratios reached record highs.

{% include figure.liquid path="assets/img/posts/uk-inflation-stickiness/fig-p02-03.png" class="img-fluid rounded z-depth-1" zoomable=true %}

When the economy reopened in 2021, pent-up consumer demand was unleashed, injecting large amounts of saved cash into the economy. However, global supply-chain disruptions severely hampered production capacity. Excess liquidity chased scarce goods, putting upward pressure on prices. Even after those disruptions eased, inflation persisted. This leads to Observation Two.

## Observation Two

Cost-push inflation is triggered by an increase in production costs (higher wages and rising raw-material prices) and a subsequent decrease in aggregate supply. Price increases in production filter to consumers, creating inflation.

Raithatha argues that UK core services, core goods, food, and energy all contribute significantly and relatively equally to inflation—a distinct pattern. In contrast, in the United States, core services dominate, while in the Eurozone, energy initially led and food later replaced it as the primary driver. The UK has faced a labour-supply shock similar to the US plus an energy-and-food shock closer to the Eurozone. This combination has produced persistently high, sticky inflation.

**Figure 6:** Bernanke and Blanchard Model of Wages and Prices (Stylised)
{% include figure.liquid path="assets/img/posts/uk-inflation-stickiness/fig-p03-01.png" class="img-fluid rounded z-depth-1" zoomable=true %}

Wage growth depends on expected inflation, labour-market tightness, desired real wages, and productivity, which drives wage inflation. Firms then set prices based on wages and costs (e.g., energy), while accounting for productivity and returns, thereby driving price inflation. This indirectly relates to Observation One: money supply influences demand, which affects labour tightness and inflation expectations. But wage-price dynamics are bidirectional. Price shocks also influence wage expectations and feed back into prices, with lags in wage adjustment and expectation formation.

**Figure 7:** Wage growth and labour-market tightness (vacancies-to-unemployment ratio), 1990-2023
{% include figure.liquid path="assets/img/posts/uk-inflation-stickiness/fig-p04-01.png" class="img-fluid rounded z-depth-1" zoomable=true %}

The vacancies-to-unemployment ratio rose over decades, correlating with significant wage growth. As the ratio rises, employers compete more intensely for scarcer workers, increasing wage pressure. Prolonged labour tightness, in absolute terms, generates faster wage growth and therefore higher inflation.

**Figure 8:** Decomposition of price inflation (2020 Q1 to 2023 Q2)
{% include figure.liquid path="assets/img/posts/uk-inflation-stickiness/fig-p04-02.png" class="img-fluid rounded z-depth-1" zoomable=true %}

Annual CPI inflation in the UK began climbing in 2021, from 0.7% at the start of the year to 5.4% by year-end. Key 2021 drivers were energy prices and shortages. Energy prices began rising in Q2 2021—before the Ukraine conflict—partly due to the Ofgem cap increase in April 2021. According to Haskel, disturbances in energy and food prices and shortages during and after the pandemic were far larger than pre-pandemic norms, and 2021 Q3 saw a sizable shortage shock.

As the Russo-Ukrainian war unfolded in Q2 2022, energy prices rose rapidly with lingering shortage effects. The UK’s inflationary response to energy shocks has been more pronounced, with different lags than the Eurozone, reflecting the UK energy market’s structure (including gas dependence) and regulatory framework. From 2022 Q3, food prices added material inflationary pressure, while tight labour-market conditions kept pushing inflation up. In early 2023, falling energy prices dragged on inflation, but high food inflation persisted. Lingering shortages and labour tightness remained positive contributors. In short, supply shocks continued to drive inflation even as lower energy prices partly offset rising food and shortage effects.

## Closing Remarks

The UK's sticky inflation stems from substantial pent-up pandemic demand unleashed when lockdowns eased, coupled with persistent supply-side shocks. Demand-pull pressures emerged as excess savings chased scarce goods when the economy reopened. Meanwhile, cost-push shocks from tight labour markets, the Russo-Ukrainian war, climate policies, and lingering pandemic shortages repeatedly battered supply.

These concurrent demand spikes and supply slumps explain why inflation remained stubbornly high despite falling at the margins. Persistently elevated production costs and embedded inflation expectations keep inflation sticky even as demand cools. Only by patiently allowing lagged policy effects to restore supply-demand balance can disinflation fully materialise.
