## Introduction

The work-from-home (WFH) revolution, facilitated by modern communication platforms like Zoom, will lead to a decline in inner cities in the short to medium run. As remote work diminishes the need for office spaces, it naturally fosters a migration of population and economic activities toward lower-density suburbs and exurbs, signaling a significant shift from traditional city centres. This paper analyses recent studies and empirical evidence, such as the decline in trips to the central business district, a negative housing price gradient, and significant welfare losses in larger cities, to demonstrate the fundamental reshaping of the urban landscape.

## The Work-From-Home Revolution

Working from home was uncommon in the United States before the COVID-19 pandemic. In 2019, only 7.2% of paid workdays were done remotely. However, the pandemic pushed the WFH rate to 61.5%, a ninefold increase from pre-pandemic levels, indicating a radical, structural shift rather than a temporary adjustment.

**Figure 1.** WFH over Time in the United States.  
*Note.* From “The Evolution of Work from Home”, by J. M. Barrero, N. Bloom, and S. J. David, 2023, *Journal of Economic Perspectives*, p. 2.

Remote work was facilitated by tools like videoconferencing (e.g. Zoom, Google Meets), file-sharing programs (e.g. Dropbox, OneDrive), collaboration platforms (e.g. Slack, Microsoft Teams), and cloud computing (Barrero et al., 2023). In addition, in 2019, the share of U.S. adults with broadband internet access at home was 73% (Pew Research Center, 2024). Collectively, these factors allowed for an overnight shift to remote work. Since the spike, the rate has cooled to 28.1% in June 2023 (Figure 1), still four times higher than pre-pandemic levels.

Barrero et al. state that WFH will stick due to several factors. First, WFH experiences have been better than expected: the average WFH productivity is 7.1% higher during the pandemic. Six in ten people report being more productive than expected, with only 14% reporting less productive. In contrast, productivity was 12% lower among employees who chose WFH before the pandemic, depending on work location (Harrington & Emanuel, 2021). Second, workers invested an average of 15 hours and $561 in home office setups for WFH. The dollar equivalent of these investments represents 0.7% of annual GDP (2019 Q4). Third, the pandemic has shifted attitudes to favour WFH. Based on others' experiences during COVID-19, two-thirds of people report improved perceptions of WFH, while under 7% see it more negatively (Barrero et al., 2021). Fourth, the proportion of new U.S. patent applications for WFH technologies doubled from January to September 2020, exceeding its previous peak. In the long run, remote work quality and effectiveness will improve.

## Inner Cities

In this paper, “inner city” primarily refers to the central business district (CBD), where offices occupy most buildings. Inner cities are characterised by dense concentrations of economic activity, businesses, jobs, infrastructure, and tall buildings, serving as hubs for information exchange, innovation, and decision-making.

### Bid-rent Theory & Supply and Demand of Urban Land

**Figure 2.** Model & Urban Bid-Rent Curve.  
*Note.* From *Land Rent Theory and Rent Curve*, by J. P. Rodrigue, 2022, (https://transportgeography.org/contents/chapter8/urban-land-use-transportation/land-rent-theory-curve/)

The bid-rent theory posits that competition near the CBD drives up land prices because proximity to economic activity maximises utility and profitability. Being closer reduces transportation and commuting costs, making central locations highly desirable for commercial and residential purposes. Consequently, as distance from the CBD increases, land use intensity, cost of land, and rent decrease (Figure 2) due to the diminishing accessibility and proximity advantages. The declining rent gradient forms the urban spatial structure, influencing the distribution of commercial, industrial, and residential areas (Rodrigue, 2022).

**Figure 3.** Urban Land Market Mechanism.  
*Note.* From *Basic Land Economics*, by J. P. Rodrigue, 2024, (https://transportgeography.org/contents/chapter8/urban-land-use-transportation/land-economics/)

As the distance from the CBD increases, the surface area of land available at that ring expands (roughly with the square of distance in a circular city model), while land near the centre is scarcer. The lower the surface area available at a given ring—or the scarcer the land—the higher the rent. Figure 3 illustrates that moving towards the CBD causes demand to rise and supply to decrease (Q2) as land is scarcest near the CBD due to limited surface area. This scarcity leads to intense competition, driving up rent (P2). More land is available farther from the centre, so competition decreases for any single parcel. Thus, land supply becomes more elastic away from the centre, causing rents to fall as availability increases (Rodrigue, 2024). This fundamental supply and demand relationship underpins land use distribution across the city, with high-rent, high-density areas near the centre and lower-rent, lower-density areas farther out.

### Reversal of Long-Standing Patterns

Remote work eliminates the necessity of commuting between different locations to access the workplace, thus depriving cities of one of their fundamental principles of organisation. In most U.S. metropolitan areas, the pandemic and the rise of WFH have decreased prices and rent in inner cities and increased with distance outwards from the CBD. This has resulted in reversing long-standing patterns. The bid-rent function's derivative has become less negative, indicating a decrease in its rate of change. Specifically, the gradient of the bid-rent curve has moderated, resulting in a scenario where the magnitude of suburban rent and price growth exceeds that of urban rent and price growth.

**Figure 4.** Bid-rent Functions for New York, San Francisco, Boston, Chicago, and Los Angeles.  
*Note.* From “Flattening the Curve: Pandemic-Induced Revaluation of Urban Real Estate”, by A. Gupta, V. Mittal, J. Peeters, and S. Van Nieuweburgh, 2021, NBER Working Paper 28675, p. 49-50.

Figure 4 illustrates the relationship between rents against distance from the city centre pre- and post-pandemic across various top metropolitan areas in the U.S. This empirical evidence confirms the flattening of the bid-rent curve. Gupta et al. highlight the surge in the appeal of suburban living, largely attributed to the increased feasibility of remote work. This has lessened the necessity for proximity to workplace hubs in city centres. According to Dingel and Neiman, 37% of jobs in the U.S. can be done remotely from home, and these jobs account for 46% of all U.S. wages, meaning they pay more relative to the 63% of jobs that cannot be done at home (Dingel & Neiman, 2020). To add, variation in remote work is a strong economic predictor of changes in rent and price gradients across the metropolitan statistical area (MSA) alone, as it explains 27.0% of the variation in rent gradient changes and 22.8% in price gradient changes. For example, within the MSA, a 10 percentage point increase in the fraction of remote work in a ZIP code correlates with a 1.8-2.9 percentage point decrease in rent growth and a 0.7-1.4 percentage point decrease in price growth (Dingel & Neiman, 2020). The flattening bid-rent curve signifies a redistribution of demand, with more households, businesses, and the overall real estate market opting for lower-density suburbs and exurbs over urban centres. Remote workers value space and comfort over the proximity to traditional economic hubs. Ramani and Bloom label this the “donut effect”: a hollowing out of CBDs and the growth of suburban outer rings (Ramani & Bloom, 2021).

**Figure 5.** Change of Address Flows in New York, SF Bay Area, Boston, and Los Angeles.  
*Note.* From “The Donut Effect of COVID-19 on Cities”, by A. Ramani and N. Bloom, 2021, NBER Working Paper 28876, p. 22, 36.

In Figure 5, the heat maps of cumulative net population inflow for metropolitan areas like New York, San Francisco Bay Area, Boston, and Los Angeles vividly capture the donut effect. These visual representations and patterns align with the flattening bid-rent functions of the corresponding cities (Figure 4 Panels A, B, C and E). Ramani and Bloom’s regression analysis reveals a clear relationship between net flows and proximity to the CBD, density, and exposure to remote work. Density and the WFH share yield negative coefficients, and distance to CBD positively correlates with net inflows. As larger metropolitan areas have higher WFH shares in their inner cities, such cities experience a larger donut effect. The largest cities in the U.S. have the highest concentration of skilled tech and finance workers exposed to WFH, and these cities, often high-priced and high-density, incentivise to relocate when given the option to WFH (Ramani & Bloom, 2021).

### Impact on Commercial Real Estate

As WFH rises, fewer workers visit the office, and since firms and businesses are always looking to cut costs, the demand for office space (commercial real estate) diminishes, decreasing the quantity of in-force leases. In addition, as the donut effect unfolds and real rent growth near the CBD declines, lease revenues have tanked by a substantial 17.38% across 105 U.S. office markets between December 2019 and December 2020. During this period, newly signed leases plummeted, increasing contractual vacancy rates to 40-year-high levels (Gupta et al., 2023).

**Figure 6.** Revenues on In-Force Leases.  
*Note.* From “Work From Home and the Office Real Estate Apocalypse”, by Gupta, A., Mittal, V., Peeters, J., and Van Nieuwerburgh, S, 2023, http://dx.doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.4124698., p. 8.

The downturn in office lease revenue, as illustrated in Figure 6, results from declining net effective rents (NER) and a reduced volume of leased space. The drop in NER, by 12.48% in 2020, was due to rent increases failing to keep pace with inflation and lower NERs in newly signed leases. Concurrently, the leased space saw a 10.93% decrease, a reflection of difficulties in leasing vacant spaces, fewer renewals, and a trend towards renewing smaller spaces (e.g. smaller Class A+ spaces) (Gupta et al., 2023). All in all, these figures highlight the significant fall in demand for office space.

The impact of the WFH shift in this context is evident. Businesses embracing remote work have significantly reduced their demand for office space over the past three years, a trend most pronounced among those with a higher proportion of remote job postings. This clearly links remote work adoption and decreased office space requirements. A trend exists amidst the decline of offices: higher-quality buildings (Class A+) either saw smaller reductions or even increases in rents, while rents for lower-quality offices declined more sharply. A logical policy response, or sometimes the only possible “conversion strategy”, for firms to attract remote workers back is to upgrade Class A-, B, and C offices to newer, Class A+ offices that have greater amenities. Therefore, due to changes in work patterns, most notably the “stickiness” of post-pandemic WFH trends (Figure 1), the long-run value of New York City office buildings is estimated to decline by 49%. The value across all U.S. office markets will face a $664.1 billion reduction over three years (Gupta et al., 2023).

**Figure 7.** Tax Incidence Before (a) and After (b) Negative Demand Shock.  
*Note.* A leftward shift of demand in a basic tax incidence model decreases equilibrium quantity and tax revenue. Author’s work.

The laws of supply and demand and the measure of tax incidence dictate that a diminishing demand corresponds to a diminishing tax revenue. As shown in Figure 7, as the demand for commercial real estate plummets (prices, rents), tax revenues also do so. Property taxes are a major funding source for urban infrastructure, public schools, and essential city services. A decline in commercial property values means less tax revenue, leading to cuts in public services and investments, making cities less attractive to businesses and residents, and further exacerbating the decline in property values, creating a downward spiral (doom loop).

## Counterargument

During the pandemic, inner cities were essentially shut down, depressing economic activity. As cities reopened, demand for inner cities returned, proving the dip in rents, prices, etc, to be temporary rather than structural. This statement has some truth. However, in the post-pandemic period, empirical evidence and recent studies support the impact of the rise of WFH in inner cities to be independent of the initial pandemic-induced impact.

Larger cities have a greater propensity to converge towards the stationary equilibrium that heavily relies on remote work than smaller cities. For instance, in New York, the pandemic resulted in a reduction of over 80% in trips to the CBD. Post-pandemic, commuting trips have stabilised at around 40% of the pre-pandemic level. In contrast, smaller cities like San Diego, CA, or Madison, WI, experienced a decline during the pandemic similar to New York's, but they have fully recovered to pre-pandemic levels (Monte et al., 2023).

**Figure 8.** Visits to the CBD Relative to January 2020.  
*Note.* From “Remote Work and City Structure”, by F. Monte, C. Porcher and E. Rossi-Hansberg, 2023, NBER Working Paper 31494, p. 31.

**Figure 9.** Distance Gradient of Housing Prices Relative to January 2020.  
*Note.* From “Remote Work and City Structure”, by F. Monte, C. Porcher and E. Rossi-Hansberg, 2023, NBER Working Paper 31494, p. 34.

Figures 8 and 9 provide a broader perspective: the largest U.S. cities, with a workforce of over 1.5 million, encountered an 80% drop in trips to the CBD during the pandemic but have now stabilised at 60% of the pre-pandemic levels. On the other hand, cities with employment lower than 150,000, which experienced a comparable decline in CBD trips during the pandemic, have now, on average, returned to pre-pandemic commuting levels (Monte et al., 2023). A negative housing price gradient (Figure 9) indicates a reduced premium for housing near the CBD. Figure 9 indicates that larger U.S. cities have relatively steep, negative distance gradients, while smaller cities have a flatter, positive gradient. Moreover, in May 2020, the densest cities comprised 55% of remote workers, compared to 23% in the least dense cities. Big, expensive, dense cities are home to the largest fraction of business service workers, who have the most exposure to remote work (Althoff et al., 2021). Therefore, according to Monte et al., larger cities, in contrast to smaller ones, experience significant long-term welfare losses due to the shift towards remote work, with the average resident in cities where CBD trips remain at 60% or less of the pre-pandemic level suffering a welfare loss of approximately 2.7%. Hence, the disparity between large and small cities in empirical evidence from recent studies reveals that remote work persists as a big-city, density-related phenomenon as the pandemic-induced pressures cool.

## Closing Remarks

This paper argues that the WFH revolution is not a temporary adjustment but a significant, enduring shift in urban landscapes, thus leading to a decline in inner cities in the short to medium run. As remote work diminishes the need for proximity to CBDs, the shift towards suburban and exurban areas is supported by recent evidence that shows a flattening of the bid-rent curve and decreased demand for commercial real estate. Furthermore, the donut effect impacts the commercial real estate sector and tax revenues of inner cities. Despite counterarguments suggesting a temporary pandemic-induced effect, sustained changes in work and living preferences indicate a structural shift, particularly in cities of the larger metropolitan areas. In conclusion, the WFH revolution necessitates reevaluating more flexible urban spaces. Adapting to these changes is essential for preserving the vitality of urban landscapes in the face of persisting remote work patterns.
